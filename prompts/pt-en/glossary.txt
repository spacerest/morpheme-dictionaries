# Prefixes
des- | undo, reverse | Works just like English "dis-" or "un-" to reverse an action or negate a quality. You'll see it all over everyday speech: desligar (to turn off), descansar (to rest/unwind), and descobrir (to discover, literally "to uncover"). Once you spot it, you start seeing it everywhere.
re- | again, back | Identical in form and meaning to English "re-", so this one is a gift. It shows up in refazer (to redo), resolver (to resolve), and recordar (to remember/recall). The meaning of repeating or returning to something is rock-solid across hundreds of words.
in-/im- | not, into | Pulls double duty just like in English: it can negate (impossível, incapaz) or indicate movement inward (incluir, importar). The form shifts to im- before p and b, exactly as it does in English "impossible" vs "inactive." Very high frequency and totally worth memorizing.
pre- | before | Same as English "pre-" and borrowed from the same Latin source. Appears in preparar (to prepare), prever (to foresee), and preferir (to prefer). The timing or priority sense is consistent and intuitive for English speakers.
sub- | under, below | Matches English "sub-" perfectly in form and meaning. You get subir (to go up, from "going up from below"), substituir (to substitute), and sublinhar (to underline). A very transparent prefix once you know to look for it.
super- | above, over, extra | Another direct cognate with English "super-", carrying the same sense of excess or superiority. Shows up in supermercado (supermarket), superar (to overcome/surpass), and superfície (surface). Easy to learn and very satisfying to recognize.
auto- | self | Borrowed from Greek and used identically to English "auto-". Appears in autocarro (bus, literally "self-car," used in European Portuguese), autoestrada (motorway), and automático (automatic). The "self-operating" idea carries through clearly.
contra- | against | A direct match to English "contra-" or "counter-". You'll find it in contradizer (to contradict), controlar (to control, from "against the roll/list"), and contrariar (to oppose). The oppositional meaning is consistent and intuitive.
ex- | former, out of | Works just like English "ex-" in both its senses: outward movement (exportar, explicar) and former status (ex-presidente). It's the same Latin root English borrowed, so the logic transfers cleanly.
# Suffixes
-ção/-são | (noun from verb) | The Portuguese equivalent of English "-tion/-sion," and one of the most productive suffixes in the whole language. It turns verbs into nouns: comunicação (communication), situação (situation), decisão (decision). If you know the English "-tion" word, the Portuguese version is often just a small spelling shift away.
-mente | (adverb marker) | This is how Portuguese makes adverbs from adjectives, just like English "-ly." Rápido becomes rapidamente (quickly), feliz becomes felizmente (fortunately/happily), and natural becomes naturalmente (naturally). It literally comes from the Latin word for "mind" or "manner," and it attaches cleanly to the feminine adjective form.
-dade/-idade | (abstract noun) | Turns adjectives into abstract nouns, matching English "-ty" or "-ity." Feliz gives felicidade (happiness), real gives realidade (reality), and livre gives liberdade (liberty/freedom). The cognates with English are thick on the ground here, making this suffix a vocabulary multiplier.
-ismo | belief, movement, style | Same as English "-ism" and borrowed from the same Greek root. You get capitalismo (capitalism), turismo (tourism), and realismo (realism). The meaning is stable and the words are nearly always transparent to English speakers.
-ista | person associated with | Matches English "-ist" and "-ista." Turns nouns and ideologies into people: jornalista (journalist), artista (artist), capitalista (capitalist). Also used for sports fans and followers in colloquial speech.
-ável/-ível | capable of, worthy of | The Portuguese version of English "-able/-ible," and it works exactly the same way. Amável (lovable/kind), possível (possible), disponível (available). The vowel before it shifts depending on the verb class, but the meaning never wavers.
-eiro/-eira | person, place, or thing | A super-productive suffix with several related meanings. It can mark a profession (enfermeiro, nurse), a tree (laranjeira, orange tree), or a container (açucareiro, sugar bowl). The occupational sense is the most useful for beginners.
-mento | (noun from verb) | Turns verbs into nouns describing a process or result, like English "-ment." Movimento (movement), tratamento (treatment), and desenvolvimento (development) all use it. A reliable pattern that mirrors English closely enough to be very learner-friendly.
-ura | quality, result | Forms nouns from adjectives or verbs, often describing a quality or a result of an action. Altura (height), leitura (reading/reading material), abertura (opening). It has a loose parallel to English "-ure" in words like "aperture" and "temperature."
-ão | augmentative | Adds a sense of bigness or intensity to a noun, and it is one of the most characteristically Portuguese morphemes. Carro becomes carrão (a big flashy car), homem becomes homenzão (a big man), and casa becomes casarão (a mansion). The tone can be admiring or mocking depending on context.
-inho/-inha | small, dear | The beloved diminutive suffix, and absolutely essential for sounding natural in Portuguese. It makes things smaller or expresses affection: cafezinho (a little coffee, the classic Brazilian term), filhinho (dear little son), casinha (a little house). It is used far more freely and warmly than English has any equivalent for.
# Roots and stems
-faz-/-fez- | make, do | The root of the incredibly high-frequency verb fazer (to do/make), which shows up in dozens of compounds and derivatives. Desfazer (to undo), refazer (to redo), satisfazer (to satisfy). Recognizing this root in disguise saves a lot of confusion.
-vel- | want, wish | The root behind querer and its relatives, but more visibly appears in the adjective suffix overlap with -ável. More usefully, velho/vela share a stem family, and voluntário (voluntary) traces to the Latin velle. Knowing the "want/will" root helps connect voltar, vontade (desire/will), and voluntário into a web of meaning.
-screv-/-scriv- | write | From Latin scribere, this root underlies escrever (to write), descrever (to describe), and inscrever (to inscribe/register). English "scribe," "describe," and "inscription" come from the same place, so the connection is immediate for most learners.
-port- | carry, bear | From Latin portare, appearing in portar (to carry/bear), transportar (to transport), importar (to import/matter), and suportar (to endure). English "portable," "transport," and "import" all share this root, making it one of the most recognizable Latin roots in both languages.
-ver- | see, truth | A rich root that underlies ver (to see), verdade (truth), verificar (to verify), and evidente (evident). The Latin root verus (true) and videre (to see) merged pathways in Portuguese. English cognates like "verify," "verdict," and "video" all connect here, making this a powerful root to internalize.
