# Prefixes
in-/im-/il-/ir- | not, un- | Works just like English "un-" or "in-", negating whatever word it attaches to. French borrowed this straight from Latin, so it feels familiar: impossible (impossible), illégal (illegal), irrégulier (irregular). The form changes to match the first letter of the root, same as in English.
dé-/dés- | undo, reverse | This prefix reverses or undoes an action, like English "de-" or "dis-". You see it constantly in everyday French: défaire (to undo), désaccord (disagreement), déplacer (to move/displace). If you know "de-" in English, you already have a head start.
re-/ré- | again, back | Means doing something again or returning to a starting point, just like English "re-". It is one of the most productive prefixes in French: refaire (to redo), revenir (to come back), répondre (to respond). You will spot this one everywhere.
en-/em- | put into, make | Turns nouns or adjectives into verbs meaning to put into a state or place, like English "en-" in "enclose" or "empower". Examples: emmener (to take away), entourer (to surround), emballer (to wrap/pack). The form is em- before b or p, just as in English.
sur- | over, above, super- | Means over, above, or in excess, matching English "sur-", "super-", or "over-". You get words like surpasser (to surpass), surcharger (to overload), surname came into English from this very prefix. It is a reliable signal that something is above or beyond the norm.
sous- | under, sub- | The mirror image of sur-, meaning under or below, like English "sub-". Think of sous-chef (the cook under the head chef), soustitre (subtitle), sous-sol (basement, literally under-ground). English borrowed sous directly in culinary contexts.
# Suffixes
-tion/-sion | (noun, action) | Turns verbs into nouns naming an action or result, identical in function to English "-tion/-sion". French and English share hundreds of these: communication, direction, permission, décision. If you see a French word ending in -tion, it is almost certainly a feminine noun and often has an English twin.
-ment | (adverb marker) | Attached to feminine adjective forms to make adverbs, working exactly like English "-ly". Lentement (slowly), vraiment (truly), facilement (easily). Once you know the pattern, you can convert most French adjectives into adverbs on the spot.
-eur/-euse | person who, -er | Marks someone who does something, like English "-er" or "-or". Un chanteur is a singer, une coiffeuse is a hairdresser, un professeur is a professor. The -euse ending is the feminine form, so the suffix also tells you the speaker's gender.
-ité/-té | quality, state, -ity | Makes abstract nouns from adjectives, matching English "-ity" or "-ty". Liberté (liberty), qualité (quality), réalité (reality). This is one of the richest sources of French-English cognates, so recognizing it gives you a huge vocabulary shortcut.
-able/-ible | able to be, -able | Means something can be done, exactly like English "-able/-ible". Mangeable (edible, literally eatable), compréhensible (comprehensible), acceptable (acceptable). Most of these map directly onto English equivalents, making them easy to guess.
-iste | person, believer, -ist | Refers to a person who practices or believes in something, matching English "-ist". Pianiste (pianist), journaliste (journalist), optimiste (optimist). It is also used for adjectives meaning someone holds a particular viewpoint.
-isme | belief, system, -ism | Names ideologies, movements, or practices, just like English "-ism". Réalisme (realism), capitalisme (capitalism), tourisme (tourism). These almost always have obvious English cognates.
-ard/-arde | (person with a tendency) | A lively, often slightly pejorative suffix marking someone known for a habit or trait. Bavard means a chatterbox (from bavarder, to chatter), fêtard means a party animal, vantard means a braggart. English borrowed it in words like drunkard and braggart.
# Roots and stems
-port- | carry | From Latin portare, this root means to carry and shows up in both French and English. Porter (to carry/wear), transport (transport), rapport (connection, literally carrying back). You already know this root from English porter, export, and import.
-venir-/-ven- | come | From Latin venire, meaning to come. It sits inside devenir (to become), prévenir (to warn or prevent), revenir (to come back), and convenir (to suit or agree). Recognizing it helps you see that all these words involve movement toward something.
-voir-/-vi-/-vu- | see | From Latin videre, meaning to see. Voir means to see, prévoir is to foresee, revoir is to see again, and évident (evident) carries the same root. The connection to English vision, view, and video makes this root very learner-friendly.
-faire-/-fais-/-fect- | do, make | From Latin facere, meaning to do or make, this is one of the most central roots in French. Faire (to do/make), défaire (to undo), satisfaire (to satisfy), and it connects to English factory, fact, and perfect. It shifts form a lot, but the core idea of doing or making stays constant.
-prendre-/-pris- | take, grasp | From Latin prehendere, to grasp or take. It lives inside comprendre (to understand, literally grasp together), apprendre (to learn), surprendre (to surprise), and entreprendre (to undertake). English surprise and entrepreneur both trace back to the same root.
-aller-/-va-/-ir- | go | The everyday verb for going, aller, has wildly irregular forms but extremely high frequency. S'en aller (to go away), y aller (to go there), and the future shortcut je vais plus infinitive all rely on it. Spotting va- or the infinitive aller tells you something about movement or direction every time.
