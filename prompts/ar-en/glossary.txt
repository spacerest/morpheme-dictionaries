# Prefixes
كِتَاب-/كُتُب- | book, writing | This root shows up everywhere in Arabic vocabulary related to writing and books. The pattern k-t-b underlies كِتَاب (kitaab, "book"), كَاتِب (kaatib, "writer"), and مَكْتَب (maktab, "office/desk"). English borrowed the related Semitic root through "Bible." Once you spot k-t-b, you know something is about writing.
اَل- | the (definite article) | This is Arabic's only definite article, like English "the," and it attaches directly to the noun. You'll see it spelled اَلْ (al-) before most consonants, but before "sun letters" like ن or ش, the ل assimilates: اَلشَّمْس becomes ash-shams, not al-shams. It's the most frequent morpheme in the language by far.
بِ-/بِالـ- | with, by, in | This little prefix is a preposition glued onto the following word. بِ- alone means "with" or "by" (بِسُرْعَة, bi-sur'a, "quickly/with speed"), and بِالـ- combines it with the definite article ("with the"). You hear it constantly in phrases like بِالْعَرَبِيَّة (in Arabic) and بِاللَّيْل (at night).
لِ-/لِلـ- | for, to (purpose) | Think of لِ- as Arabic's "for" or "to," showing purpose or recipient. لِ- attaches before indefinite nouns (لِوَلَدٍ, "for a boy") and لِلـ- before definite ones (لِلْبَيْت, "for the house"). You'll see it in لِمَاذَا (limaadha, "why," literally "for what") all the time.
مَ-/مِ- | place/instrument of | This prefix turns a root into a place or tool word, and it's extremely productive. مَكْتَب (maktab) is "office" (place of writing), مَدْرَسَة (madrasa) is "school" (place of studying), مَطْبَخ (matbakh) is "kitchen" (place of cooking). Spot a م at the start and think "place where this root-action happens."
تَ- | (reflexive/Pattern V) | When you see تَ- prefixed onto a verb pattern, it often signals a reflexive or middle-voice meaning, similar to adding "-self" in English. تَعَلَّمَ (ta'allama) means "he learned" (reflexively acquiring knowledge), versus عَلَّمَ (he taught someone else). It's a clue that the subject is acting on or for itself.
اِسْتَ- | to seek, to request | This prefix marks a very recognizable verb pattern (Pattern X) meaning "to seek to do" or "to consider something as." اِسْتَفْهَمَ means "to seek understanding/inquire," اِسْتَقْبَلَ means "to receive/welcome" (seek to face), and اِسْتَخْدَمَ means "to use/employ." When you see ista- at the start of a verb, think "seeking or deeming."
اِنْ- | (passive/reflexive Pattern VII) | This prefix signals Pattern VII verbs, which are typically passive or spontaneous in meaning. اِنْكَسَرَ (inkasara) means "it broke" (by itself), versus كَسَرَ (kasara, "he broke it"). اِنْفَتَحَ means "it opened up," اِنْتَهَى means "it ended/came to an end." The action happens to the subject rather than being done by it.
# Suffixes
-ة | (feminine marker) | This letter, taa' marbuuta, is the most reliable femininity signal in Arabic. It sits at the end of words and is usually pronounced as a soft "a" or "at." مُدَرِّس (mudarris) is "male teacher," مُدَرِّسَة (mudarrisa) is "female teacher." It also forms abstract nouns like حُرِّيَّة (hurriyya, "freedom") and مَدِينَة (madiina, "city").
-ِيَّة | (abstract noun / adjective) | This suffix creates abstract nouns and adjectives of belonging, much like English "-ity" or "-ness." عَرَبِيَّة (arabiyya) means "Arabic/Arabness," حُرِّيَّة (hurriyya) means "freedom," and وَطَنِيَّة (wataniyya) means "nationalism/patriotism." You'll also see it in country-related adjectives like مِصْرِيَّة (Egyptian, fem.).
-ُون/-ِين | (masculine plural) | These are the two forms of the masculine sound plural suffix, used for people and some things. -ُون appears in the nominative case (مُعَلِّمُون, "teachers"), and -ِين in other cases (مُعَلِّمِين). Most learner Arabic collapses these into -iin in everyday speech. Compare مُسْلِم (Muslim, singular) and مُسْلِمُون (Muslims, plural).
-اَت | (feminine plural) | This suffix is the feminine plural ending, strikingly regular and easy to spot. سَيَّارَة (sayyaara, "car") becomes سَيَّارَات (sayyaaraat, "cars"), مَعْلُومَة (ma'luuma, "piece of information") becomes مَعْلُومَات (ma'lumaat). It attaches cleanly to the singular base and is often used for loanwords too, like تِلِفُونَات (telephones).
-ِي | my / (nisba adjective) | This suffix does double duty. Attached to a noun it means "my": كِتَابِي (kitaabi, "my book"), بَيْتِي (bayti, "my house"). It also forms nisba adjectives of origin or belonging: مِصْرِي (misri, "Egyptian"), عَرَبِي (arabi, "Arab/Arabic"). Context makes the meaning clear, and the suffix is pronounced the same way in both uses.
-كَ/-كِ | your (singular) | These are the "your" suffixes, -كَ for masculine and -كِ for feminine addressees. كِتَابُكَ means "your book" (to a man), كِتَابُكِ means "your book" (to a woman). Arabic marks gender on the listener, not just the speaker, which surprises English speakers. You'll hear these constantly in greetings like شُكْراً لَكَ (shukran laka, "thank you, masc.").
-هُ/-هَا | his / her | These third-person possessive suffixes attach to nouns to mean "his" (-هُ) and "her" (-هَا). بَيْتُهُ means "his house," بَيْتُهَا means "her house." They also serve as direct object pronouns on verbs: رَأَيْتُهُ (ra'aytuhu, "I saw him"), رَأَيْتُهَا (ra'aytuha, "I saw her"). Two jobs, one tiny suffix.
# Roots and stems
ع-ل-م | knowledge, learning | This three-letter root is one of the most productive in Arabic. عِلْم (ilm) is "knowledge/science," عَالِم (aalim) is "scholar/scientist," مَعْلُومَة (ma'luuma) is "a piece of information," and تَعَلَّمَ (ta'allama) is "he learned." The root connects to "Islam" through عَالَم (world) and underlies dozens of academic and everyday words.
د-ر-س | studying, lessons | From this root you get مَدْرَسَة (madrasa, "school"), دَرَسَ (darasa, "he studied"), دَرْس (dars, "lesson"), and مُدَرِّس (mudarris, "teacher"). The root traveled into English as "madrassa." Whenever you see د-ر-س arranged in a word, it's almost certainly talking about studying or education.
ق-و-ل | saying, speech | This root covers speaking and saying. قَالَ (qaala) is "he said," قَوْل (qawl) is "a saying," and مَقُولَة (maquula) is "a quote/saying." You'll also see it in the phrase قُلْ (qul, "say!") which opens famous Quranic verses. Recognizing this root helps you spot verbs of speech quickly.
ذ-هَ-ب | going, gold | This root primarily means "to go": ذَهَبَ (dhahaba, "he went"), يَذْهَب (yadhhabu, "he goes"), مَذْهَب (madhhab, "direction/school of thought"). Interestingly, ذَهَب also means "gold" as a separate noun from the same root (the thing you "go for"?). It's a great high-frequency verb root to internalize early.
ج-ل-س | sitting, sessions | أَجْلِس (ajlis, "sit down!"), جَلَسَ (jalasa, "he sat"), جُلُوس (juluus, "sitting"), مَجْلِس (majlis, "council/sitting room"). The word "majlis" has entered English as a loanword for a council or assembly room. Once you know this root, you'll recognize official meeting names across Arabic-speaking countries.
ك-ل-م | words, speaking | كَلَامَ (kalaam) means "speech/words," كَلِمَة (kalima) means "a word," تَكَلَّمَ (takallama) means "he spoke," and مُكَالَمَة (mukaалама) means "a phone call" (literally a mutual speaking). This root is essential for any conversation about language, words, or communication, which makes it useful from day one.
ع-م-ل | work, doing | عَمَلَ (amala) means "he worked," عَمَل (amal) means "work/deed," عَامِل (aamil) means "worker/factor," and مَعْمَل (ma'mal) means "laboratory/workshop." The root is close to عِمَارَة (imaara, "building/architecture") and shows up in words about practical activity and labor across registers.
ر-ج-ع | returning, going back | رَجَعَ (raja'a) is "he returned," رُجُوع (rujuu') is "return/going back," and اِرْتِجَاع (irtijaa') is "retrieval/reversal." You'll hear يَرْجِع (yarji', "he returns/goes back") constantly in everyday directions and instructions. This root also underlies the idea of referring back to something.
و-ج-د | finding, existing | وَجَدَ (wajada) means "he found," يُوجَد (yuujad) means "there is/it exists," وُجُود (wujuud) means "existence," and مَوْجُود (mawjuud) means "available/present/existing." This root handles both "finding" and "being/existing" in Arabic, combining what English expresses with two different verbs. Very high frequency in daily speech.
ح-ب-ب | love, liking | أَحَبَّ (ahabba) means "he loved," حُبّ (hubb) is "love," حَبِيب (habiib) is "beloved/darling," and مَحْبُوب (mahbuub) means "beloved/popular." Habiib is probably one of the first Arabic words English speakers encounter through music and media. The doubled ب is a signature of this root and of the Pattern II intensive verb form.
