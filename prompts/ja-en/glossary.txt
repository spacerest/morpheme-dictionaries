# Prefixes
お-/ご- | honorific/polite | This prefix is added to nouns and some verbs to make speech more polite or to refer respectfully to someone else's things. お- attaches to native Japanese words (おみず, おてら) while ご- tends to attach to Sino-Japanese words (ごかぞく, ごりょうしん). Think of it like adding "your esteemed" in front of something, though in everyday use it just softens and polishes the word.
不- | not, un- | Borrowed from Chinese, this prefix negates the word that follows it, very much like English "un-" or "in-". You'll see it in words like ふあん (anxiety, literally "not peaceful"), ふべん (inconvenient), and ふまじめ (unserious). It stacks neatly onto Sino-Japanese adjectives and nouns, so once you spot it, you can often guess the word's meaning right away.
無- | without, -less | Another negating prefix from Chinese that means "lacking" or "without," similar to English "-less" or "non-". It shows up in むりょう (free of charge, "without fee"), むりょく (powerless), and むだ (wasteful, "without purpose"). It tends to signal a complete absence of something rather than just a reversal, so it feels a bit stronger than 不-.
非- | non-, anti- | This prefix marks something as outside a category or contrary to a norm, like English "non-" or "anti-". You'll find it in ひじょうしき (lack of common sense), ひこうしき (unofficial, "non-formal"), and ひにんげんてき (inhuman). It often appears in more formal or written contexts, giving a slightly official tone.
再- | re-, again | This prefix means doing something again, mapping almost perfectly onto English "re-". It appears in さいかい (resuming, meeting again), さいりよう (reuse), and さいせい (regeneration or playback). Learners who already know "re-" in English will find this one clicks immediately, and it's very productive in modern Japanese.
# Suffixes
-的 | -like, -ish, (adjectival) | Attached to Sino-Japanese nouns to turn them into na-adjectives, this suffix works like English "-ic," "-al," or "-like." You'll see it in かがくてき (scientific), せかいてき (global), and きほんてき (fundamental). It's extremely common in written and spoken Japanese, so recognizing it lets you unlock a huge number of adjectives from nouns you already know.
-化 | -ize, become | This suffix turns a noun into "the process of becoming that thing," paralleling English "-ize" or "-ification." Examples include きかい化 (mechanization), デジタル化 (digitalization), and こくさい化 (internationalization). It's a very productive morpheme in news and academic language, and once you know it, headlines become a lot easier to read.
-者 | person who | This suffix means "a person who does or is associated with something," much like English "-er," "-ist," or "-or." You can spot it in いしゃ (doctor), きしゃ (journalist), and かんこうきゃく... well, more directly in がくしゃ (scholar) and かんりしゃ (manager). It's a reliable signal that the word refers to a person in a role.
-語 | language, word | Attached to a country or group name, this suffix means "the language of," like English "-ese" or "-ish." You know it from にほんご (Japanese), えいご (English), and フランスご (French). It can also mean "word" in compounds like たんご (vocabulary word) and げんご (language in general), making it doubly useful.
-中 | in the middle of, throughout | As a suffix, this means something is currently happening or ongoing, similar to English "in the course of" or "-ing." You'll see it in じゅぎょうちゅう (during class), えいぎょうちゅう (open for business, "in operation"), and かんがえちゅう (thinking, "mid-thought"). It also appears as a prefix or standalone word meaning "inside" or "throughout," so it's a very flexible morpheme.
-方 | way, how to | This suffix turns a verb stem into a noun meaning "the way of doing" something, like English "-ing" as a gerund or the phrase "how to." Examples are たべかた (way of eating), つかいかた (how to use something), and かんがえかた (way of thinking). It's incredibly useful for asking how things are done and appears constantly in instruction manuals and everyday conversation.
-屋 | shop, person who sells | Originally meaning a building or shop, this suffix identifies either a store or the person who runs it. You'll see やおや (greengrocer), パンや (bakery/baker), and ほんや (bookstore/bookseller). It has a warm, everyday feel, more associated with small neighborhood shops than big chain stores.
-費 | cost, expense | This suffix marks a word as referring to a type of expense or fee, similar to English words ending in "-age fee" or "cost of." You'll find it in せいかつひ (living expenses), こうつうひ (transportation costs), and がくひ (tuition). Spotting this suffix in a context about money immediately tells you the topic is expenditure.
-員 | member, staff | This suffix indicates a member of a group or organization, similar to "-er," "-man," or "staff" in English compound nouns. Common examples are かいしゃいん (company employee), てんいん (shop staff), and かいいん (member of an organization). It's a reliable marker that the word refers to someone who belongs to or works within an institution.
# Roots and stems
-時 | time, o'clock | This root appears both as a suffix meaning "o'clock" (いちじ, さんじ) and within words meaning "time" or "occasion," like じかん (time in general) and どうじ (simultaneous, "same time"). Recognizing じ as a time marker helps enormously when telling time, reading schedules, and understanding when things happen.
-気 | spirit, energy, feeling | This root carries a sense of energy, atmosphere, or mental/physical state, loosely comparable to the concept of "vibe" or "mood" in English. You'll find it in げんき (healthy, energetic), てんき (weather, "sky-energy"), and やるき (motivation, "will-energy"). It's a core Japanese concept and appears in so many everyday words that tuning in to it changes how you feel the language.
-人 | person, people | One of the most common and productive roots in Japanese, this character means "person" and appears in a vast number of words. You know it from にほんじん (Japanese person), がいこくじん (foreigner), and じん alone as a suffix for nationality. When read as ひと it means "person" more individually, but as じん or にん it tends to appear in compounds about roles, nationalities, and groups.
-前 | before, in front | This root means both spatial "in front of" and temporal "before," making it doubly useful. You'll find it in まえ alone (in front), でんしゃのまえ (in front of the train), ごぜん (AM, "before noon"), and なまえ (name, literally "what stands before you"). Recognizing it lets you decode directions, times, and several set phrases all at once.
-心 | heart, mind, feeling | This root refers to the inner emotional or mental world, similar to how English uses "heart" metaphorically. It appears in こころ (heart/mind as a standalone word), あんしん (relief, "peaceful heart"), しんぱい (worry, "heart trouble"), and しんり (psychology, "mind-reason"). Japanese uses this root where English might say "feeling," "mind," or "soul" depending on context.
-力 | power, ability, force | This root marks words related to strength, capability, or influence, much like English "-power" or "-force" in compounds. You'll see it in ちから (power or strength on its own), きょうりょく (cooperation, "united force"), のうりょく (ability, "brain-power"), and たいりょく (physical stamina). It's a satisfying root to learn because it immediately makes compound words feel logical and memorable.
