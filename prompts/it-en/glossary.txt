# Prefixes
in-/im-/il-/ir- | not, un- | Works just like English "in-" meaning "not" -- think "inactive" or "impossible." Italian follows the same pattern of adjusting the spelling before certain consonants: *impossibile* (impossible), *illegale* (illegal), *irregolare* (irregular). Very handy because many of these words are near-identical to English ones.
ri- | again, re- | The Italian twin of English "re-," meaning to do something again. You'll see it constantly in everyday speech: *ritornare* (to return), *ricominciare* (to start again), *rileggere* (to reread). Once you spot it, you can often guess the meaning of a new verb instantly.
s- | reversal, away | This little prefix often undoes or reverses the meaning of the base word, a bit like English "un-" or "dis-." It turns *fortuna* (luck) into *sfortuna* (bad luck) and *fare* (to do) into *sfare* (to undo). It can also just intensify or shift meaning slightly, so context always helps.
pre- | before | Identical in spelling and meaning to English "pre-," making this one of the easiest prefixes to learn. You get *preparare* (to prepare), *prevedere* (to foresee), and *precedente* (previous). English borrowings from Latin give you a big head start here.
dis- | apart, not | Another close match to English "dis-," expressing negation or separation. Compare *disordine* (disorder), *disappunto* (disappointment), and *discutere* (to discuss). The similarity to English makes these words very learner-friendly.
super- | above, over | Exactly like English "super-," this prefix expresses something above or beyond the norm. You get *supermercato* (supermarket), *superiore* (superior/upper), and *superate* (to overcome). Italian teenagers also use it as slang for "awesome," just like in English.
con-/com-/col-/cor- | together, with | Matches English "con-/com-" and signals togetherness or joining. Think *comunicare* (to communicate), *collaborare* (to collaborate), *correre* (to run, but also to converge). Recognizing this prefix unlocks a huge family of Latin-rooted words shared with English.
anti- | against | Spelled and used almost identically to English "anti-." You get *antibiotico* (antibiotic), *antipatico* (unpleasant, literally "against sympathy"), and *antincendio* (fire-prevention). A very reliable prefix to know.
extra- | outside, beyond | Another prefix that travels perfectly between Italian and English. *Extraterrestre* (extraterrestrial), *straordinario* (extraordinary, where "extra" contracted to "stra-"), and *extracurriculare* (extracurricular). Watch for the contracted form "stra-" in common words too.
# Suffixes
-zione/-sione | action, result (noun) | The Italian counterpart of English "-tion/-sion," and it works the same way -- turning verbs into nouns that describe an action or its result. *Comunicazione* (communication), *decisione* (decision), *situazione* (situation). If you can say it in English with "-tion," there's a very good chance the Italian version exists with "-zione."
-mente | (adverb marker) | This suffix turns adjectives into adverbs, exactly the way "-ly" works in English. *Veloce* (fast) becomes *velocemente* (quickly), *ovviamente* (obviously), *assolutamente* (absolutely). It's wonderfully regular -- just attach it to the adjective and you're done.
-ità/-tà | quality, state (noun) | The Italian version of English "-ity" or "-ty," forming abstract nouns from adjectives. *Città* (city), *università* (university), *qualità* (quality), *libertà* (liberty). Many of these are direct cognates with English, so this suffix instantly expands your vocabulary.
-bile | able to be (adjective) | Lines up neatly with English "-ble" or "-able/-ible," meaning something is capable of being done. *Possibile* (possible), *incredibile* (incredible), *disponibile* (available). Spot this ending and you know you're dealing with a quality or capability word.
-ismo | belief, movement (noun) | Identical to English "-ism" and used in the same contexts -- ideologies, movements, styles. *Capitalismo* (capitalism), *turismo* (tourism), *realismo* (realism). These words are almost always transparent to an English speaker.
-ista | person who does/believes (noun) | The partner of "-ismo" and equivalent to English "-ist." *Artista* (artist), *pianista* (pianist), *turista* (tourist). Importantly, "-ista" is the same form for both masculine and feminine -- gender shows up in the article instead.
-ore/-tore | person who does (noun) | Corresponds to English "-or/-tor" as in actor or director. *Attore* (actor), *direttore* (director), *professore* (professor). These are mostly straightforward cognates and a very common pattern for naming occupations or agents.
-ezza | quality, state (noun) | Forms abstract nouns from adjectives, a bit like English "-ness." *Bellezza* (beauty/beautifulness), *tristezza* (sadness), *dolcezza* (sweetness). There's no single English suffix that matches it perfectly, but once you know it you'll see it everywhere in emotional and descriptive vocabulary.
-ino/-ina | small, diminutive | A beloved Italian suffix that makes things smaller or cuter -- sometimes just more affectionate. *Caffè* becomes *caffellatte* and *cappuccino*, *casa* (house) can become *casina* (little house). English doesn't have a direct equivalent, but it works like adding "little" before a word. It's used in everyday speech all the time.
-mente | (adverb marker) | Duplicate removed -- see above entry.
-mento | action, result (noun) | Turns verbs into nouns describing a process or result, similar to English "-ment." *Movimento* (movement), *pagamento* (payment), *appartamento* (apartment, literally a separation). Lots of cognates with English here, which makes them easy to absorb.
# Roots and stems
-port- | carry | From Latin *portare*, this root means "to carry" and is shared across Italian and English. *Portare* (to carry/to bring), *importare* (to import/to matter), *esportare* (to export). Once you know this root you can break apart a whole cluster of verbs and nouns.
-scriver-/-script- | write | From Latin *scribere*, this root underlies writing-related words in both languages. *Scrivere* (to write), *descrivere* (to describe), *iscrizione* (inscription/enrollment). English "describe" and "inscription" are close cousins, making this root feel familiar.
-vid-/-vis- | see | From Latin *videre*, giving us words about sight and vision in both Italian and English. *Vedere* (to see), *visibile* (visible), *televisione* (television). The connection to English "video," "vision," and "visit" (originally "to go to see") makes this root very intuitive.
-prender-/-pres- | take, grasp | From Latin *prehendere*, this root shows up in common Italian verbs and also in English borrowings. *Prendere* (to take), *comprendere* (to understand, literally "to grasp together"), *apprendere* (to learn/apprehend). English "comprehend" and "apprehend" are the same root, just dressed differently.
-ven-/-vien- | come | From Latin *venire*, this is one of the most productive roots in Italian. *Venire* (to come), *avventura* (adventure, something that comes toward you), *convenire* (to agree/suit, literally "to come together"). English "advent," "convention," and "event" all share this ancient root.
