# Prefixes
ge- | (past participle marker) | This is one of the most important prefixes in Dutch. It wraps around verbs to form past participles, much like English "-ed" does. You will see it constantly: "gemaakt" (made), "gegeten" (eaten), "gelopen" (walked). The tricky part is that some verbs skip it entirely, but for regular verbs it is your best friend.
be- | makes verb transitive | This prefix turns intransitive verbs or nouns into transitive verbs, similar to how English sometimes uses "be-" in words like "befriend." It often adds a sense of acting on something: "betalen" (to pay), "bezoeken" (to visit), "beginnen" (to begin). It is extremely common and gives many Dutch verbs their characteristic shape.
ver- | change of state / away | Dutch "ver-" is a workhorse prefix with a few overlapping meanings: transformation, movement away, or intensification. Think of it loosely like English "for-" in "forget" or the German cognate "ver-." Examples include "veranderen" (to change), "vergeten" (to forget), and "vertellen" (to tell). Spotting it helps you recognize that something is shifting or becoming.
ont- | removal / becoming | This prefix signals undoing or the start of something, paralleling English "un-" or "de-." It can mean stripping something away or the onset of a state: "ontdekken" (to discover), "ontvangen" (to receive), "ontsnappen" (to escape). Think of it as peeling something back or kicking something off.
on- | not / un- | Directly equivalent to English "un-" or "in-," this prefix negates adjectives and some nouns. It is completely transparent once you know it: "onmogelijk" (impossible), "ongelukkig" (unhappy), "onbekend" (unknown). If you see "on-" at the start of a Dutch word, the meaning is almost certainly the opposite of the base word.
her- | again / re- | This prefix maps almost perfectly onto English "re-," meaning to do something again. It is very recognizable and consistent: "herhalen" (to repeat), "herinneren" (to remember), "hergebruiken" (to reuse). Whenever you want to say something is done over or anew, "her-" is the prefix to reach for.
mee- | along / with | Derived from "mee" (along, with), this prefix adds the sense of joining in or accompanying an action. It behaves like English "along" tacked onto a verb: "meebrengen" (to bring along), "meekomen" (to come along), "meewerken" (to cooperate). It shows up heavily in spoken everyday Dutch and is very handy for social situations.
af- | off / away / completion | This separable prefix carries the idea of moving off, finishing, or detaching, similar to English "off" in phrasal verbs. It appears in: "afmaken" (to finish), "afzetten" (to drop off / to turn off), "afhalen" (to pick up / to collect). Because Dutch is rich in separable verbs, "af-" splits off and migrates to the end of the clause in main sentences.
uit- | out / finished | Like English "out" in phrasal verbs such as "work out" or "run out," this prefix conveys outward movement or completion: "uitgaan" (to go out), "uitleggen" (to explain, literally lay out), "uitverkocht" (sold out). It is one of the most frequent separable prefixes and gives Dutch verbs a vivid, directional feel.
# Suffixes
-ig | having the quality of | This suffix turns nouns into adjectives, much like English "-y" (sandy, cloudy). It is extremely productive: "handig" (handy, skillful), "gelukkig" (happy, lucky), "langzaam" becomes an adjective family through similar patterns. You will see "-ig" everywhere on Dutch adjectives and it almost always signals a descriptive quality.
-heid | -ness / -hood | This is the go-to suffix for forming abstract nouns from adjectives, directly mirroring English "-ness." It is completely regular and highly productive: "vrijheid" (freedom, literally free-ness), "schoonheid" (beauty), "eerlijkheid" (honesty). Whenever you want to name a quality or state in Dutch, slap "-heid" on the adjective and you are almost certainly right.
-lijk | -ly / -like | This suffix forms adjectives meaning "in the manner of" or "characteristic of," and it also creates many adverbs. It is a cognate of English "-ly" and German "-lich": "vriendelijk" (friendly), "waarschijnlijk" (probably, likely), "persoonlijk" (personal). Many Dutch adjectives you encounter in formal or everyday speech will end in "-lijk."
-ing | action / result | Just like English "-ing" used as a noun (a meeting, a building), Dutch "-ing" turns verbs into nouns describing an action or its result. Examples: "vergadering" (meeting), "regeling" (arrangement, regulation), "opleiding" (education, training). This is one of the most frequent noun-forming suffixes in Dutch and you will bump into it on every page of text.
-er/-aar | person who does | These two variants form agent nouns, exactly like English "-er" in "teacher" or "worker." The choice between them depends on the verb ending, but the meaning is always the same: "leraar" (teacher), "werker" (worker), "bakker" (baker). If you see a Dutch noun ending in "-er" or "-aar," there is a good chance it names a person defined by what they do.
-schap | -ship / collective state | This suffix creates abstract nouns denoting a condition, relationship, or collective, just like English "-ship" in "friendship" or "partnership." Examples: "vriendschap" (friendship), "maatschappij" has the same root, "lidmaatschap" (membership), "wetenschap" (science, knowledge). It gives a formal or collective flavor to the concept.
-loos | without / -less | Directly equivalent to English "-less," this suffix negates nouns to mean lacking something: "werkloos" (unemployed, literally work-less), "dakloos" (homeless), "zinloos" (pointless, senseless). It is completely transparent and consistent, making it one of the easiest Dutch suffixes to learn and apply productively.
# Roots and stems
-werk- | work / craft | This root is shared between Dutch and English and appears in many compound nouns describing activities or structures. "Werk" on its own means work, and it combines freely: "huiswerk" (homework), "kunstwerk" (artwork), "samenwerken" (to work together). Recognizing it instantly unlocks a large family of everyday words.
-huis- | house / building | Another direct cognate with English, "huis" means house and appears as a compounding root in dozens of common nouns. "Ziekenhuis" (hospital, literally sick-house), "postkantoor" shares the building idea, "warenhuis" (department store). Dutch loves compound nouns and "huis" is one of the most frequent building blocks you will encounter.
# Linking elements
-s- | (linking element) | In Dutch compound nouns, an "-s-" is often inserted between two parts purely to aid pronunciation, with no meaning of its own. It is similar to the linking "-s-" in English words like "sportsman." You see it in "rijbewijs" but more clearly in "regeringsgebouw" (government building) or "tijdschrift" (magazine, literally time-writing). Do not try to interpret it as a genitive or plural -- it is just glue.
-e-/-en- | (linking element) | Like the "-s-" linker, "-e-" or "-en-" appears between parts of compound nouns as a connecting sound, sometimes echoing an old genitive ending. Examples: "kinderwagen" (pram, literally children-wagon), "staatsburger" (citizen, literally state-citizen), "dagelijks" uses a similar pattern. Seeing these linkers helps you break compounds apart and read Dutch long words without panic.
