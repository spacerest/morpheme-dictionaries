# Prefixes
des- | undo, reverse | Works just like English "dis-" or "un-" to reverse or undo something. You probably already know "desastre," and you can see it in action in words like "deshacer" (to undo), "descansar" (to rest, i.e. un-tire yourself), and "desaparecer" (to disappear). Once you spot it, you can often guess what a word means by stripping it off.
re- | again, back | Identical in spelling to English "re-" and carries the same meaning. "Regresar" (to go back), "repetir" (to repeat), and "recordar" (to remember, literally re-cord back to the heart) all use it. It's one of the safest morphemes to transfer directly from your English intuition.
in-/im- | not, into | Does double duty just like in English: it can negate (as in "imposible," "incorrecto") or mean movement inward ("incluir," "importar"). The form changes to "im-" before b and p, exactly as in English "impossible" vs. "incorrect." Recognizing which sense is meant usually comes from context.
pre- | before | Straight cognate with English "pre-." You'll see it in "preparar" (to prepare), "presentar" (to present), and "prevenir" (to prevent). Spanish borrowed heavily from Latin alongside English, so these feel very familiar.
sub- | under, below | Same as English "sub-" in "subway" or "submarine." Spanish uses it in "subir" (to go up, from below), "subterráneo" (underground), and "subtítulo" (subtitle). The meaning is consistently spatial or hierarchical.
ex- | out of, former | Means "out of" in words like "explicar" (to explain, draw out) and "exportar" (to export), and "former" in "ex-novio" (ex-boyfriend). This is a very safe cognate prefix that transfers directly from English.
entre- | between, among | Comes from Latin "inter-" and matches English "inter-" in meaning. You see it in "entrar" (to enter, go between inside), "entregar" (to hand over), and "entrenar" (to train). It's also used on its own as a preposition meaning "between."
con-/com- | with, together | The same Latin root that gives English "co-," "com-," and "con-." It signals togetherness or completeness in "comprender" (to understand, grasp together), "conectar" (to connect), and "construir" (to build together). The form shifts to "com-" before b and p.
# Suffixes
-ción/-sión | (noun from verb) | The Spanish equivalent of English "-tion" and "-sion," and it's enormously productive. "Comunicación," "solución," "expresión" are all direct cognates. If you know an English "-tion" word, there's a very good chance the Spanish version ends in "-ción." The accent always falls on the final syllable.
-mente | (adverb marker) | Tacked onto the feminine form of an adjective to make an adverb, exactly like English "-ly." "Rápidamente" (quickly), "fácilmente" (easily), "completamente" (completely). Once you know an adjective, you can almost always create the adverb for free this way.
-ado/-ada | (past participle / adjective) | Marks the past participle of -ar verbs and doubles as an adjective. "Cansado" (tired), "enamorado" (in love), "terminado" (finished). English has a looser version of this in words like "confused" or "interested," which also started as past participles.
-ido/-ida | (past participle of -er/-ir verbs) | The partner of -ado for -er and -ir verbs. "Comido" (eaten), "vivido" (lived), "perdido" (lost). Together with -ado, these two endings let you recognize hundreds of adjectives and compound tenses instantly.
-ista | person who does/believes | Same as English "-ist," indicating a person associated with a practice or ideology. "Artista" (artist), "periodista" (journalist), "turista" (tourist). Conveniently, these are the same in both genders -- just change the article.
-dad/-tad | (abstract noun) | Turns adjectives into abstract nouns, just like English "-ity" or "-ty." "Libertad" (liberty), "ciudad" (city), "realidad" (reality). Many of these are direct cognates with English "-ty" words and carry the same meaning.
-mente-less -ero/-era | person or place related to | Marks someone who makes or deals with something, or a place associated with it. "Panadero" (baker), "enfermero" (nurse), "carretera" (highway, road-place). It's close in spirit to English "-er" for occupations, though not a perfect match.

"Hablar" (to speak), "comer" (to eat), "vivir" (to live). Recognizing these endings instantly flags a word as a verb in its base form, which is how verbs are listed in dictionaries.

# Roots and stems
-hab- | have, hold | From Latin "habere," this root shows up in "haber" (to have, auxiliary), "habitual" (habitual), and "habilidad" (ability, what you have mastered). English "have" and "habit" are distant cousins of this same root.
-scrib-/-script- | write | The same Latin root behind English "scribe," "script," and "description." In Spanish you'll find "escribir" (to write), "descripción" (description), and "manuscrito" (manuscript). A reliable bridge between the two languages.
-port- | carry | Latin "portare" is behind English "transport," "import," and "portable," and the same root runs through Spanish "portar" (to carry), "transportar" (to transport), and "reportar" (to report). The meaning of movement or carrying is consistent across both languages.
-dict- | say, tell | From Latin "dicere," giving English "dictate," "predict," and "verdict," and Spanish "decir" (to say), "predicar" (to preach), and "dedicar" (to dedicate). The connection between speaking and this root is easy to remember.
-vis-/-vid- | see | The root behind English "vision," "video," and "evident," and Spanish "ver" (to see), "visitar" (to visit), "televisión" (television), and "evidente" (evident). The two forms reflect different Latin conjugations but share the same core meaning of sight.
-aud- | hear | Latin "audire" gives English "audible," "audience," and "audio," and Spanish "escuchar" shows its influence alongside direct borrowings like "auditorio" (auditorium) and "audífono" (hearing aid / earphone). A great root to anchor listening-related vocabulary.
-loc- | place | From Latin "locus," the same root in English "location" and "local." In Spanish, "lugar" (place) derives from it, as do "local" (local), and "colocar" (to place, to put). It's a tidy root for building spatial vocabulary.
-form- | shape, form | Directly shared between English and Spanish with the same spelling and meaning. "Forma" (shape/form), "informar" (to inform), "uniforme" (uniform), and "transformar" (to transform) all carry the idea of shape or structured arrangement. One of the easiest roots to recognize.
