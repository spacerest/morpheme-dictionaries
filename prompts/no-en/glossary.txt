# Prefixes
u-/un- | not, un- | Works just like English "un-" to flip a word negative. You'll spot it constantly: "uheldig" (unlucky), "umulig" (impossible), "uvanlig" (unusual). If you know the positive form, slapping u- on the front almost always gives you the opposite.
be- | (makes verb transitive) | This prefix turns nouns and adjectives into verbs, or gives existing verbs a more direct object feel. It's related to the English prefix "be-" as in "befriend." Examples: "betale" (to pay), "besøke" (to visit), "begynne" (to begin). It's one of the most common verb-forming tools in Norwegian.
for- | (intensifier / away) | A versatile prefix that can mean something is done wrongly, excessively, or away. Parallels English "fore-" or "for-" in words like "forbid." Examples: "forklare" (to explain), "forberede" (to prepare), "forsvinne" (to disappear). The exact flavor shifts with context, but you'll learn to feel it quickly.
an- | on, at, toward | Shows direction or initiation of an action, similar to how English uses "on-" in "onset" or "onset." You see it in "ankomme" (to arrive), "ansette" (to hire), "antyde" (to suggest). It often implies starting or directing something at a target.
over- | over, too much | Maps almost perfectly onto English "over-" meaning above or in excess. Great one to know because it's highly predictable: "overraske" (to surprise), "overnatte" (to stay overnight), "overta" (to take over). If something is overdone or above something else, over- is probably there.
ut- | out | The Norwegian cousin of English "out-" in words like "outrun" or "output." It signals movement outward or completion: "utføre" (to carry out), "utvikle" (to develop), "utgang" (exit). Pairs beautifully with verbs of motion and action.
sam- | together, co- | Think English "co-" or "together." It signals collaboration or combination: "sammen" (together), "samfunn" (society, literally "community"), "samarbeide" (to cooperate). It's the glue that joins things, and you'll find it all over social and civic vocabulary.
mis- | wrong, mis- | A direct parallel to English "mis-" meaning done incorrectly or badly. Very recognizable for English speakers: "misforstå" (to misunderstand), "mislykkes" (to fail/go wrong), "mistanke" (suspicion). Safe to assume it signals something has gone sideways.
# Suffixes
-lig | -ly, -like | Attaches to nouns and adjectives to mean "having the quality of," much like English "-ly" or "-like." It's one of the most productive adjective suffixes in Norwegian: "vennlig" (friendly), "farlig" (dangerous), "tydelig" (clear/obvious). Once you know it, you'll be able to guess dozens of adjectives.
-het | -ness, -ity | Turns adjectives into abstract nouns, exactly like English "-ness" or "-ity." It's wonderfully regular: "frihet" (freedom/liberty), "sikkerhet" (safety/security), "mulighet" (possibility). Spot an adjective, add -het, and you've got the noun for that quality.
-ing | (action noun) | Forms nouns from verbs, just like English "-ing" in "running" or "building." It's extremely common and predictable: "møte" becomes "møting" isn't standard, but "trening" (training), "mening" (meaning/opinion), "læring" (learning). The verb root usually points you straight to the meaning.
-er | (agent / person) | Marks someone who does something, parallel to English "-er" in "teacher" or "worker." It's also the plural ending for many nouns, so context matters: "lærer" (teacher), "arbeider" (worker), "baker" (baker). One of those suffixes you'll encounter on literally every page.
-else | (abstract noun) | Creates abstract nouns from verbs, often referring to a state or process. It's a bit like English "-ance" or "-ence" but more broadly used: "følelse" (feeling), "forståelse" (understanding), "betydelse" (meaning/significance). Slightly more formal-sounding than -ing nouns.
-ig | having, full of | Similar to -lig but often more directly means "characterized by" something, like English "-y" or "-ous." Examples: "lykkelig" (happy), "rolig" (calm), "billig" (cheap). There's overlap with -lig and the two work similarly, so native intuition develops fast.
-skap | -ship, -hood | Forms nouns about relationships, states, or collectives, just like English "-ship" in "friendship" or "-hood" in "neighborhood." It's very transparent: "vennskap" (friendship), "medlemskap" (membership), "kunnskap" (knowledge). The English cognate "-ship" makes this one especially easy to remember.
# Roots and stems
-ferd- | journey, travel | This root carries the idea of movement or travel and shows up across a range of common words. It connects to English "fare" as in "farewell" or "wayfarer." You'll find it in "ferdsel" (traffic/passage), "adferd" (behavior, literally "manner of going"), and "reiseferd" (journey). Recognizing it helps unlock a cluster of motion-related vocabulary.
-arbeid- | work | The core root for work in Norwegian, directly related to English "labor" via Germanic roots. It appears in some of the most essential everyday words: "arbeid" (work), "arbeider" (worker), "arbeidsplassen" (the workplace). Once you know this root, the whole family of work-related words opens up.
-sted- | place | Means "place" and is essentially the same word as English "stead" in "instead" or "homestead." It combines freely with other words: "hjemsted" (hometown), "arbeidssted" (workplace), "nettsted" (website). Spotting it tells you immediately that a location or spot is being named.
# Linking elements
-s- | (genitive linker) | This little connector appears between the parts of compound words to show possession or connection, much like the English "'s" but built right into the word without an apostrophe. You see it in "statsminister" (prime minister, literally "state's minister"), "barnebok" (children's book), "arbeidsdag" (workday). It's a small sound with a big structural job in Norwegian compounding.
-e- | (compound linker) | A short vowel used to smooth the join between two parts of a compound word, with no strong independent meaning of its own. Think of it like a hyphen made of sound: "høyesterett" (supreme court), "dagslys" (daylight). It keeps compound words from piling up too many consonants and makes them easier to say.
