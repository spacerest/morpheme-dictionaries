# Prefixes
без-/бес- | without, -less | Works just like English "without" or the suffix "-less." You'll see без- before voiced consonants and бес- before voiceless ones -- the same kind of sound-matching English does informally. Examples: безопасный (safe, lit. "without danger"), бесплатный (free of charge), бесконечный (endless).
в-/во- | into, in- | Signals movement into something or an inward action, close to English "in-" or "into." The во- form appears before tricky consonant clusters to make pronunciation easier. Examples: входить (to enter), вносить (to bring in), вдыхать (to inhale).
вы- | out, out- | The mirror image of в-, expressing outward movement or direction, like English "out-" or "ex-." It's one of the most reliable prefixes in Russian -- if you see вы-, something is coming out. Examples: выходить (to exit), выносить (to carry out), выдыхать (to exhale).
за- | behind, start of action | Has two main flavors: location behind something, or the beginning of an action (like English "start to..."). Context usually makes it clear. Examples: забыть (to forget), заговорить (to start speaking), закрыть (to close).
на- | onto, onto surface | Suggests placement on a surface or completion, parallel to English "on." It also frequently marks a perfective verb, signaling the action is finished. Examples: написать (to write, completed), надеть (to put on clothing), находить (to find).
не- | not, un- | The straightforward Russian "not," sticking to the front of adjectives, nouns, and verbs just like English "un-" or "non-." It's one of the first prefixes worth memorizing because it appears absolutely everywhere. Examples: небольшой (not big), неправда (untruth), невозможный (impossible).
пере- | re-, over-, across | Covers a lot of ground, much like English "re-" or "over-." It can mean doing something again, going across something, or overdoing it. Examples: перечитать (to reread), переехать (to move/cross over), перепить (to drink too much).
по- | a little, begin briefly | Often softens a verb to mean doing something for a short while or just a bit, a nuance English handles with phrases like "for a bit." It's also the most common perfectivizing prefix. Examples: поговорить (to have a chat), пойти (to set off), посмотреть (to take a look).
под-/подо- | under, sub- | Means under or approaching from below, matching English "under-" or "sub-" almost perfectly. The подо- form appears before certain consonant clusters. Examples: подземный (underground), подойти (to approach), поддержать (to support, lit. "hold under").
про- | through, pro- | Conveys passing through something or a thorough completion of an action, similar to English "through" or "pro-." It often implies the action went all the way. Examples: прочитать (to read through/finish reading), пройти (to pass through), проверить (to check/verify).
раз-/рас- | un-, dis-, apart | Signals separation, undoing, or distribution, very close to English "un-" or "dis-." Like без-/бес-, it switches between раз- and рас- depending on the following sound. Examples: разговор (conversation, lit. "talking apart"), расписание (schedule), разрушить (to destroy).
с-/со- | together, down from | Works in two directions: joining things together (like English "co-/com-") or movement downward and off a surface. со- appears before clusters. Examples: собрать (to gather/collect), сделать (to do/make, completed), сойти (to step down).
# Suffixes
-ость | (abstract noun) | Turns adjectives into abstract nouns, doing exactly what English "-ness" or "-ity" does. Once you know this suffix, you can build dozens of nouns from adjectives you already know. Examples: возможность (possibility), свобода... actually: скорость (speed), радость (joy), личность (personality).
-ник/-ница | (person noun) | Marks a person associated with something, like English "-er" or "-ist." The -ница form is the feminine version. You'll notice it in tons of everyday words for people and roles. Examples: работник (worker), ученик (student/pupil), помощник (helper/assistant).
-ный/-ная/-ное | (adjective ending) | The most common Russian adjective suffix, functioning like English "-al," "-ful," or "-ic." The ending changes to agree with noun gender -- -ный (masc.), -ная (fem.), -ное (neut.). Examples: красивый... and: интересный (interesting), удобный (comfortable), опасный (dangerous).
-ть/-ти | (infinitive marker) | The standard infinitive ending, equivalent to English "to" in "to run" or "to speak." Every Russian verb has it, so spotting it instantly tells you you're looking at a base verb form. Examples: говорить (to speak), идти (to go), любить (to love).
-ение/-ание | (verbal noun) | Turns verbs into nouns describing a process or result, just like English "-ing," "-tion," or "-ment." It's incredibly productive and you'll run into it on almost every page of Russian text. Examples: решение (solution/decision), задание (assignment), объяснение (explanation).
-ка | (diminutive/colloquial noun) | A super-common suffix that makes things smaller, cuter, or just more colloquial, similar to English "-ie" or "-y" (as in "doggie"). Russians use it constantly in casual speech. Examples: книжка (little book/book), остановка (bus stop), девочка (little girl).
-ов/-ев/-ин | (possessive/genitive adj.) | Forms adjectives meaning "belonging to" or "of," somewhat like English "'s" or adjectives from proper nouns. You see it a lot in surnames and place names. Examples: мамин (mom's), Пушкин (Pushkin, from a root meaning "cannon"), отцов (father's).
# Roots and stems
-говор- | speak, talk | This root is all about speaking and talking, related to the old Slavic word for "speech." You can recognize it across a whole family of words and it has a distant cousin in English "garble." Examples: говорить (to speak), разговор (conversation), договор (contract/agreement).
-дел- | do, make | Pops up in words about doing and making things, loosely parallel to Latin "fac-" in English words like "factory." It also appears in words about dividing. Examples: делать (to do/make), дело (business/matter/deed), отдел (department/section).
-ид-/-йд- | go (on foot) | The core root for going and walking, appearing in a huge family of motion verbs. It's one of the most important roots to recognize because motion verbs are everywhere in Russian. Examples: идти (to go/walk), выйти (to exit), найти (to find).
-люб- | love, like | Means love or fondness, cognate with words in other Slavic languages and distantly echoing Latin "libido." It forms both everyday and formal words. Examples: любить (to love), любовь (love), любимый (beloved/favorite).
-работ- | work | The root behind all things work-related, with a fascinating history -- it's related to the word that became "robot" in Czech and then English. Examples: работать (to work), работа (work/job), работник (worker/employee).
-сказ-/-скаж- | say, tell, story | Tied to speaking and storytelling, this root shows up in words for saying, tales, and fairy stories. It's a good one to know because it links everyday speech verbs to literary vocabulary. Examples: сказать (to say), сказка (fairy tale), рассказ (short story/tale).
-вид-/-виж- | see | Core root for seeing and vision, cognate with Latin "vid-" (as in "video"). Appears across a wide family of perception verbs and nouns. Examples: видеть (to see), видимый (visible), очевидный (obvious, lit. "clearly seen").
-знa- | know | The root for knowledge and familiarity, with a distant connection to English "know" and Latin "gno-." It underlies both knowing facts and being acquainted with people. Examples: знать (to know), знакомый (familiar/acquaintance), признать (to recognize/admit).
-нес-/-нос- | carry, bear | Covers carrying and bearing — physical transport and also abstract meanings like enduring. The stem alternates: нес- in perfectives/past, нос- in imperfective present. Examples: нести (to carry), носить (to carry/wear), принести (to bring).
-ним-/-нять- | take, grasp | The root of taking and grasping; it's one of the most productive roots in Russian, appearing in dozens of common verbs with different prefixes. Examples: понять (to understand), принять (to accept), снять (to take off/rent).
-пис- | write | Related to Latin "script-" and English "inscribe." Shows up in all writing-related words. Examples: писать (to write), написать (to write/finish writing), список (list).
-ход-/-хож- | walk, go | A highly productive motion root meaning general movement or walking. Often pairs with directional prefixes. Examples: ходить (to walk/go), выходить (to exit), входить (to enter), переход (crossing/transition).
-чит- | read, count | Covers both reading and counting — a single root that branched into two meanings. Examples: читать (to read), считать (to count/consider), прочитать (to read through).
