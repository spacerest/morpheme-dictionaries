# Prefixes
nie- | not, un- | This is Polish's all-purpose negation prefix, working almost exactly like English "un-" or "not." Slap it onto an adjective or noun and you flip the meaning: "dobry" (good) becomes "niedobry" (bad/not good), "możliwy" (possible) becomes "niemożliwy" (impossible). It's one of the most reliable prefixes in the language and you'll see it constantly.
prze- | through, over, re- | A workhorse prefix with a few related senses: movement through something, doing something excessively, or doing it again. "Czytać" (to read) becomes "przeczytać" (to read through/finish reading), "chodzić" (to walk) gives "przechodzić" (to pass through). Think of the English prefix "trans-" or "over-" as rough cousins.
po- | after, a little, (perfectivizer) | One of the most common prefixes you'll meet, it can signal that an action is completed, done briefly, or done after something else. "Iść" (to go) becomes "pójść" (to set off), "czekać" (to wait) becomes "poczekać" (to wait a bit). It also forms adverbs from adjectives, like "polsku" meaning "in Polish."
na- | onto, (perfectivizer) | Expresses motion onto a surface or completion of an action, much like English "on-" or "up." "Pisać" (to write) becomes "napisać" (to write up/complete writing), "uczyć" (to teach) becomes "nauczyć" (to teach successfully). You'll often see it signal that something got fully done.
od- | away from, off, back | Mirrors English "away-" or "off-" very neatly. "Chodzić" gives "odchodzić" (to walk away/leave), "pisać" gives "odpisać" (to write back, to reply). It can also mean returning something or undoing an action, similar to English "re-" in words like "return."
wy- | out, forth | Corresponds closely to English "out-" and signals movement outward or completion to the point of exhaustion. "Chodzić" becomes "wychodzić" (to go out, to exit), "pić" (to drink) becomes "wypić" (to drink up completely). It's related to the same root as "vy-" in other Slavic languages.
za- | behind, beginning, (perfectivizer) | One of the trickiest because it covers a lot of ground: starting an action, moving behind something, or completing it. "Śpiewać" (to sing) becomes "zaśpiewać" (to start singing/sing a piece), "mknąć" gives "zamknąć" (to close, literally to move behind). Just knowing it often signals "beginning of an action" will take you far.
roz-/roze- | apart, spreading out | Think of English "dis-" or "un-" when something comes apart or spreads. "Mawiać" gives "rozmawiać" (to converse, literally to talk things out), "umieć" gives "rozumieć" (to understand, literally to spread one's knowing). The alternate form "roze-" appears before some consonant clusters.
bez- | without | A direct parallel to English "without-" or the prefix "un-/in-" in the sense of lacking something. "Praca" (work) gives "bezrobocie" (unemployment, literally without-work), "sens" gives "bezsensowny" (senseless). It's pronounced like "bez" (the plant elder) and is completely predictable in meaning.
współ- | together, co- | The Polish equivalent of English "co-" or "fellow-," marking joint or shared activity. "Pracować" (to work) gives "współpracować" (to cooperate), and the noun form "współpraca" means cooperation. You can hear a distant cousin in the English word "social" if you trace far enough back through Proto-Indo-European.
# Suffixes
-ość | (abstract noun) | Turns adjectives into abstract nouns, just like English "-ness." "Wolny" (free) becomes "wolność" (freedom/liberty), "możliwy" becomes "możliwość" (possibility). It's one of the most productive suffixes in the language and once you know it, you can generate and recognize dozens of nouns immediately.
-owy/-owa/-owe | (adjective from noun) | This is the go-to suffix for turning nouns into adjectives, roughly like English "-al" or "-ic." "Telefon" becomes "telefoniczny" but simpler words use "-owy": "dom" (house) gives "domowy" (domestic/home-), "woda" (water) gives "wodny" (aquatic). The ending changes to match gender: "-owy" for masculine, "-owa" for feminine, "-owe" for neuter.
-ek/-ka | (diminutive) | Polish speakers love diminutives, and this suffix is the most common way to make something smaller or express affection. "Kot" (cat) becomes "kotek" (little cat/kitty), "dom" (house) becomes "domek" (little house, cottage). English has traces of this in words like "droplet" or "duckling" but Polish uses it far more freely and warmly.
-nia/-alnia | place where | Marks a place associated with an activity or thing, like English "-ery" or "-ry." "Piec" (to bake) gives "piekarnia" (bakery), "sypiać" (to sleep) gives "sypialnia" (bedroom). Once you know this pattern, reading signs and room names becomes much easier.
-arz/-arz | person who does | Marks a person by their occupation or habitual activity, like English "-er" or "-or." "Piec" gives "piekarz" (baker), "pisać" gives "pisarz" (writer). It's related to the Latin "-arius" suffix that also gave English words like "secretary" and "notary," so there's a genuine historical connection.
-anie/-enie | (verbal noun) | Turns a verb into a noun describing the action itself, functioning like English "-ing" when used as a noun. "Czytać" (to read) gives "czytanie" (reading), "mówić" (to speak) gives "mówienie" (speaking). These are very common in formal and written Polish and let you talk about activities in the abstract.
# Roots and stems
-mów-/-maw- | speak, say | This root sits at the heart of words about speaking and language. "Mówić" is the basic verb (to speak), "rozmowa" is a conversation, "wymowa" is pronunciation. It's related to the root in "Slavic" words for "word" and shows up in compounds constantly. Knowing it helps you recognize a whole family of communication words.
-prac- | work | The core root for work and labor. "Praca" is the noun (work, job), "pracować" means to work, "pracownik" is an employee. It doesn't have a direct English cognate but the pattern is crystal clear once you know the root, and you'll see it everywhere from job ads to office signs.
-chod-/-jd- | go, come, walk | This root underlies a huge family of motion verbs in Polish. "Chodzić" is to walk/go (habitually), and then prefixes transform it: "wychodzić" (to go out), "przychodzić" (to come/arrive), "odchodzić" (to leave), "podchodzić" (to approach). Recognizing this root unlocks a core system of Polish movement vocabulary all at once.
-wied-/-wiad- | know, tell | Related to knowledge and telling, this root connects "wiedzieć" (to know facts), "powiedzieć" (to say/tell), and "wiadomość" (a message or piece of news). It shares a deep Indo-European ancestor with English "wit" and "wise," so there is a genuine etymological kinship across thousands of years.

# Prefixes
prze- | through / over | This prefix works a lot like English "over-" or "trans-" -- it suggests going through something, doing it too much, or transforming it completely. You'll see it in "przeczytać" (to read through), "przepisać" (to rewrite/copy), and "przejść" (to pass through or cross). Once you spot it, you'll notice it everywhere.
do- | to / up to / finish | Think of this as pointing toward a goal or completion, similar to how English uses "up to" or "through to the end." It shows up in "dojść" (to reach, arrive on foot), "dostać" (to get/receive), and "dokończyć" (to finish up). It often adds a sense of completing an action.
w-/we- | in / into | This is the Polish equivalent of English "in-" when it means movement inward. The "we-" form appears before clusters that would be hard to pronounce otherwise. You'll find it in "wejść" (to enter), "wziąć" (to take), and "włożyć" (to put in). It's short but powerful.
u- | away / completion | This prefix is tricky because it does double duty -- it can suggest moving away, or it can mark that an action is fully completed. Compare "uciec" (to flee, escape) and "ugotować" (to cook something through). You'll also see it in "uczyć się" (to learn/study). Context is your friend here.
s-/z-/ze- | together / down / completion | These are all surface forms of the same prefix, and which one appears depends purely on the sound that follows it. It often marks perfective aspect (a completed action) or suggests bringing things together. See it in "zrobić" (to do/make, completed), "zejść" (to come down), and "zebrać" (to collect/gather).
przed- | before / pre- | A near-perfect match with English "pre-" or "fore-," this prefix places something earlier in time or space. You'll recognize it in "przedszkole" (preschool, literally "before-school"), "przedmiot" (subject/object, thing placed before), and "przedwczoraj" (the day before yesterday). Very learner-friendly.
pod- | under / sub- | This lines up neatly with English "under-" or "sub-." It can mean physically beneath something or a lesser/subordinate version. Look for it in "podpisać" (to sign, literally "write under"), "podróż" (journey, travel), and "podłoga" (floor, that which is underfoot). Solid and consistent.
# Suffixes
-ić/-yć | (infinitive marker) | These are the endings that mark a verb as an infinitive -- the base dictionary form. "-ić" follows soft consonants and "-yć" follows hard ones, but they mean the same thing. You'll see them on thousands of verbs: "mówić" (to speak), "robić" (to do), "słyszeć" (to hear). Think of it as the Polish equivalent of English "to."
-ka | (feminine diminutive / noun) | One of Polish's hardest-working suffixes, "-ka" forms feminine nouns, often with a slightly smaller or more everyday feel than the base word. It appears in "córka" (daughter), "książka" (book), and "herbatka" (a nice cup of tea, from "herbata"). It can also be a straight feminine agent noun ending.
-ek/-ik | (masculine diminutive) | The masculine counterpart to "-ka," this suffix makes nouns smaller, cuter, or just more colloquial. Poles use diminutives constantly in everyday speech, far more than English speakers do. You'll hear "stolik" (little table), "kotek" (kitten/dear cat), and "chłopiec" from the same family. Paying attention to these will help you sound natural fast.
-nia | place / facility | When you see "-nia" at the end of a word, think "a place where that thing happens." It's like English "-ery" or "-ry" in "bakery" or "laundry." Look for "kawiarnia" (café, coffee place), "piekarnia" (bakery), and "kuchnia" (kitchen). Very useful for talking about places in a city.
-stwo | state / group / (abstract noun) | This suffix forms abstract nouns about states, conditions, or collective groups, similar to English "-hood," "-ship," or "-ness" in some contexts. See "państwo" (state/country, also Mr. and Mrs. together), "towarzystwo" (company/society), and "małżeństwo" (marriage/married couple). It often has a slightly formal or official feel.
-cie | (verbal noun / event) | This ending creates nouns out of verbs, naming the act or event itself, and it's one of the ways Polish parallels English "-ing" used as a noun. You'll meet it in "spotkanie" (meeting, from "spotkać"), "mieszkanie" (apartment, literally a dwelling), and "wyjście" (exit, the act of going out). Very common in everyday life.
# Roots and stems
-myśl- | think / thought | This root sits at the heart of words about thinking and ideas, related to the old Slavic word for thought. You'll find it in "myśleć" (to think), "pomyśleć" (to think something over), and "przemyślany" (well thought-out, deliberate). It's a great root to know because thinking comes up in conversation constantly.
-pisz-/-pis- | write / writing | From the same ancient root that gives Russian "pisat'" and relates distantly to the Latin family of "script," this root covers all things written. Look for it in "pisać" (to write), "napisać" (to write/compose something), and "przepisać" (to copy or rewrite). Once you know it, you'll spot it in "podpis" (signature) too.
-daj-/-da- | give | This root means giving or handing something over, and it's incredibly productive in Polish. You'll see it in "dawać" (to give, imperfective), "dać" (to give, perfective), "podać" (to pass/hand over), and "wydać" (to spend money or publish). It links to the same ancient Slavic root as Russian "davat'."
-czyt-/-czyt- | read | The core root for reading in Polish, showing up reliably across related words. "Czytać" means "to read," "przeczytać" means to read something all the way through, and "czytelnik" is a reader (as in a library patron). It's a phonetically distinctive cluster that you'll start to recognize quickly once you've seen it a few times.
-now-/-naw- | new / renew | This root connects to the adjective "nowy" (new) and carries the idea of newness or renewal when it appears in verbs and nouns. You'll see it in "nowość" (novelty/new thing), "odnowić" (to renovate/renew), and "ponownie" (once again, anew). Handy because "new" is such a common concept.
-robi-/-rob- | do / make | Built from "robić" (to do or make), this root is one of the most fundamental in everyday Polish. It appears in "zrobić" (to make/do, completed), "praca" shares conceptual space with it in many learner minds, and "przerobić" means to redo or process. Think of it as the Polish workhorse verb, much like "make" or "do" in English.
-słów-/-słow-/-sław- | word / glory | This root has two related but distinct lives: in words about language it means "word" (from "słowo"), and in names and some words it carries the old Slavic meaning of glory or fame. You'll see the word-meaning in "słownik" (dictionary, literally word-book) and "słownictwo" (vocabulary), and the glory-meaning in names like "Stanisław" and "Sławomir."
