# Prefixes
an- | not, un- | Works like English "un-" to negate adjectives and nouns. It often causes lenition (a h after the first consonant) on the word that follows. You'll see it in words like "anáil" (breath, from "an-" + root meaning breathe), "anord" (disorder), and "anróiteach" (wretched). A very handy reversal tool once you spot it.
ath- | re-, again | Parallel to English "re-", this prefix means doing something over again. It lenites the following consonant. You'll find it in "athdhéanamh" (remaking), "athrú" (change, literally re-moving), and "athair" (father, though that one is etymological rather than productive). Very useful for talking about repetition.
comh- | co-, together | Related to English "co-" and Latin "com-", this means together or mutually. It often shortens to "co-" in modern spelling. You see it in "comharsa" (neighbor), "comhrá" (conversation, talking together), and "comhoibriú" (cooperation). A great clue that something involves people acting jointly.
dí- | un-, de-, remove | Signals removal or reversal, similar to English "de-" or "un-". It appears in "dífhostaíocht" (unemployment, literally de-employment), "díol" (to sell, pay off), and "díshealbhú" (dispossession). When you see it up front, something is being taken away or undone.
do- | hard to, bad | Signals difficulty or badness, the opposite of "so-". Think of it as a cousin to English "dys-" or "mis-". You get "dodhéanta" (impossible, hard to do), "dochreite" (unbelievable), and "doiligh" (difficult). Pair it with "so-" and you can contrast easy versus hard versions of things.
droch- | bad, wicked | A strongly negative prefix meaning bad or poor quality, similar to English "mis-" or "ill-". It lenites what follows. You see it in "drochshláinte" (bad health), "drochíde" (ill-treatment), and "drochuair" (a bad time, misfortune). Very expressive and commonly heard in everyday speech.
ear-/éar- | mis-, un-, negative | A less common but recognizable negative or privative prefix, related to the word "éagóir" (injustice). It appears in "éagóir" (injustice), "éagumas" (inability), and "éagnaí" (unwise person). Worth knowing because it marks a whole cluster of words about absence or wrongness.
fo- | under, sub- | Means under or secondary, just like English "sub-" or "under-". It stays fairly consistent across words. You will spot it in "fo-éadaí" (underwear, under-clothes), "fotheanga" (sublanguage, dialect), and "foghlaim" (learning, picking up from under someone). A useful spatial and hierarchical marker.
frith- | anti-, counter- | Direct equivalent of English "anti-" or "counter-". It marks opposition to something. You see it in "frithghníomh" (counteraction), "frithbheartaíocht" (countermeasure), and "frithghiniúint" (contraception). Learners of European languages will find this very familiar in feel.
iar- | after, post-, ex- | Means after in time or former in status, like English "post-" or "ex-". It lenites what follows. You will encounter it in "iarchéim" (postgraduate), "iardhátú" (post-dating), and "iarmhairt" (consequence, after-effect). Knowing this one helps you talk about sequences and former roles.
idir- | inter-, between | Matches English "inter-" almost perfectly. It signals something happening between parties or things. Examples include "idirghníomhú" (interaction), "idirnáisiúnta" (international), and "idirlíon" (the Internet, between-network). A very modern and productive prefix in Irish.
in- | in, into, (intensifier) | Borrowed and native uses overlap here. As a native prefix it can intensify or mean "very", and in loans it echoes English "in-". You see it in "inúsáidte" (usable), "inchurtha" (comparable), and "inite" (edible, able to be eaten). The pattern "-able" in English often maps onto "in-" plus verbal noun in Irish.
mí- | bad, mis-, wrong | One of the most common negative prefixes. Like English "mis-" it marks something done wrongly or a bad state. It lenites what follows. You find it in "míshásta" (unhappy), "mícheart" (incorrect), and "míthuiscint" (misunderstanding). Very high frequency and easy to peel off once you know it.
neamh- | non-, un-, without | A straightforward negation prefix very similar to "non-" in English. It lenites what follows. Common in "neamhspleách" (independent, non-dependent), "neamhfhoirmiúil" (informal), and "neamhchoitianta" (unusual). Learners often find this one of the clearest and most predictable prefixes in the language.
ró- | too, over- | Means excessive, just like English "too" or "over-". It lenites what follows and is instantly recognisable in speech. You hear it in "ró-bheag" (too small), "rómhór" (too big), and "róluath" (too fast). One of the first prefixes learners naturally start using because it maps so directly onto everyday English complaint.
so- | easy to, good | The positive partner of "do-", meaning easy or good quality, like English "eu-" or "bene-". Found in "soghluaiste" (mobile, easy to move), "soléite" (readable, easy to read), and "sochreidte" (believable). Pairing "so-" and "do-" together is a nice way to learn contrasting vocabulary at once.
sean- | old, former | Means old or former, equivalent to English "old" used as a prefix or "ex-". It lenites what follows and appears everywhere. You see it in "seanbhean" (old woman), "seanfhocal" (proverb, literally old word), and "seanathair" (grandfather). Very high frequency and a key part of talking about age and tradition.
super-/sár- | super-, excellent | "Sár-" is the native Irish intensifying prefix meaning excellent or top-level, matching English "super-" or "arch-". It lenites what follows. Found in "sármhaith" (excellent, super-good), "sárscannán" (blockbuster film), and "sárobair" (superb work). A great little prefix for expressing enthusiasm about quality.
# Suffixes
-acht/-eacht | (abstract noun) | The most important nominalizing suffix in Irish, turning adjectives and verbs into abstract nouns like English "-ness", "-ity", or "-hood". "Maith" (good) becomes "maitheas" but the pattern is clearest in "Éireannacht" (Irishness), "cairdiúlacht" (friendliness), and "saíocht" (wisdom). Once you know this ending, you can guess the meaning of dozens of abstract nouns.
-adh/-eadh | (verbal noun) | The standard verbal noun ending for many verbs, functioning like English "-ing" or "-tion". Irish uses verbal nouns constantly in place of infinitives. You see it in "moladh" (praising/recommendation), "ceannach" (buying), and "glacadh" (accepting, taking). Recognizing it helps you see that a noun and a verb action are the same word wearing different hats.
-aí/-í | (agent, plural) | This suffix marks a person who does something (like English "-er" or "-ist") and also forms certain plurals. "Múinteoir" has its own ending but the "-í" pattern gives you "amhránaí" (singer), "rincí" (dancer forms), and "ceoltóirí" (musicians, plural). It is extremely common in everyday vocabulary for professions and roles.
-áil | (verbal noun of action) | A very common verbal noun suffix, especially for borrowed verbs and everyday activities. It functions like the "-ing" form. You get "glanadh" in some dialects but also "seiceáil" (checking), "páirceáil" (parking), and "íoslódáil" (downloading). Spotting "-áil" at the end of a word is a strong clue that this is an action or activity noun.
-ánach/-ánach | person from, member of | Marks a person associated with a place or group, like English "-an" in "Parisian" or "-er" in "New Yorker". You see it in "Corcaíoch" (person from Cork), "Albanach" (Scotsman), and "Sasanach" (Englishman). It is a very natural suffix to learn early because talking about nationalities and origins is so common.
-as | (abstract noun state) | A noun-forming suffix that creates words for states or conditions, overlapping with "-acht" but often felt as more classical. It appears in "iontas" (wonder, amazement), "brónnas" (sorrow), and "cumhacht" (power, though the root shifts). Recognizing "-as" endings helps group a big family of emotional and state words.
-each/-ach | (adjective forming) | One of the main adjective-forming suffixes, like English "-ful", "-ous", or "-y". It turns nouns into descriptive adjectives. "Uisce" (water) gives "uisceach" (watery), "áthas" (happiness) gives "áthasach" (happy), and "dúshlán" (challenge) gives "dúshlánach" (challenging). Immensely productive and a key pattern to lock in early.
-ín | little, dear (diminutive) | The beloved Irish diminutive suffix, adding smallness or affection like "-kin" or "-let" in English, and well known from English loanwords like "colleen" (girl, from "cailín"). You see it in "cailín" (girl), "bábóg" from base plus diminutive layers, and "gairdín" (garden, little enclosure). Often carries a warm or familiar tone.
-iúil/-úil | (adjective: having quality of) | A highly productive adjective suffix meaning having the quality of, equivalent to English "-ful" or "-ly". Very common in modern and everyday vocabulary. You find it in "cairdiúil" (friendly), "áitiúil" (local), and "dúchasúil" (indigenous, native). Once you recognize this ending, a huge range of descriptive adjectives become much easier to decode.
-lann | place, hall, room | Marks a dedicated building or place for a specific activity, like English "-ery" or "-ium". It is transparent and very learnable. You see it in "leabharlann" (library, book-place), "pictiúrlann" (cinema, picture-place), and "ospidéal" for hospital though "-lann" is the clearer pattern in "bialann" (restaurant, food-place). Extremely useful for talking about public places.
-óir/-eoir | (agent noun: person who) | The main suffix for making agent nouns, exactly like English "-er", "-or", or "-ist". Very high frequency. You get "múinteoir" (teacher), "dochtúir" (doctor, borrowing the pattern), "tiománaí" versus "ceoltóir" (musician). It is one of the first suffixes learners encounter because job titles and roles are essential vocabulary.
-ú | (verbal noun, change of state) | Another key verbal noun suffix, often used for verbs describing change or process. Functions like English "-ing" or "-ment". Common in "athrú" (changing), "lagú" (weakening), and "cruthú" (proving, creating). Together with "-adh" and "-áil" this forms the core toolkit of verbal nouns that Irish uses instead of infinitives.
# Roots and stems
-bean-/-mná- | woman | The root for woman, appearing as "bean" in the nominative and "mná" in other forms, which is why you see both shapes. It is in "bean" (woman), "mná" (women, genitive), and compounds like "bean tí" (woman of the house, housewife). The alternating forms can confuse learners at first but they are the same root.
-ceart- | right, correct, justice | Means right or correct and appears in many everyday words. It is related to the Latin "rectus" and English "correct" via the Indo-European family. You find it in "ceart" (right, justice), "mícheart" (wrong), and "ceartúchán" (correction). Recognizing this root pulls together a cluster of words about correctness and fairness.
-cloch-/-cloich- | stone | The root for stone, appearing in different case forms. It is foundational in placenames and everyday words. You see it in "cloch" (stone), "cloicheann" (skull, literally stone-head), and countless Irish placenames like "Clochán" (stepping stones). The stone-related vocabulary is rich and often poetic in Irish.
-déan- | make, do | One of the most essential verb roots in Irish. "Déan" means to make or do, and it appears in the irregular verb "déanaim" (I make/do), "déanta" (done, made), and "déantús" (manufacture). It is slightly irregular but so common that learners absorb its forms quickly from exposure.
-fad-/-fhad- | long, length | Carries the meaning of length or duration. You see it in "fada" (long), "i bhfad" (far away, literally in length), and "go fóill" (yet, for a while). Recognizing this root helps with both spatial and time-related vocabulary, since Irish uses length metaphors for both distance and duration.
-geal- | white, bright | Means white or bright and appears in some of the most common and emotionally resonant words in Irish. You find it in "geal" (bright, white), "soilsigh" (to illuminate) and related forms, and beautifully in "mo ghile mear" (my bright darling, from a famous song). Also visible in "Nollaig" (Christmas, related to bright/holy day roots).
-maith- | good | The root for goodness and moral positive value, parallel to English "good" or "well". Appears constantly in greetings and everyday speech. You see it in "maith" (good), "go maith" (well, good), "ní maith liom" (I don't like), and "maithiúnas" (forgiveness). This is one of the first roots learners internalize simply through daily greetings.
-muir-/-mara- | sea | The root for sea, appearing in different grammatical forms. Irish has a rich seafaring vocabulary and this root is everywhere. You find it in "muir" (sea, formal/poetic), "farraige" (sea, common spoken word from the same area of meaning), and "Muir Éireann" (the Irish Sea). Also visible in "murúch" (mermaid) and placenames all along the coast.
-teach-/-tí- | house, home | The root for house or home, with the genitive form "tí" appearing constantly in compounds and phrases. You see it in "teach" (house), "bean tí" (woman of the house), "tigh" (older spelling variant), and "cónaí" phrases. Placenames all over Ireland contain versions of this root, making it essential for both geography and everyday domestic vocabulary.
-teanga- | language, tongue | Means both tongue (the body part) and language, just like English "tongue" in its older sense. You find it in "teanga" (language/tongue), "teangacha" (languages, plural), and "fo-theanga" (sublanguage). It is a great root to know because language-learning itself becomes much more fun when you can talk about language in the target language.
