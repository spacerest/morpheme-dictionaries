# Prefixes
be- | makes verb transitive | This prefix turns many nouns and adjectives into transitive verbs, much like English "be-" in "befriend" or "bewilder." It is extremely common in everyday Danish and usually adds a sense of affecting or covering something. Examples: betale (pay), beskrive (describe), begynde (begin).
for- | away/amiss/intensive | One of the busiest prefixes in Danish, it can signal doing something wrong, doing it away, or simply intensifying, depending on the verb. English has a distant cousin in "fore-" and "for-" in words like "forbid." Examples: forstå (understand), forlade (leave/abandon), forbyde (forbid).
ud- | out | Mirrors English "out-" almost perfectly, giving a sense of outward movement or completion. You will spot it constantly in spoken Danish because Danes love phrasal-verb-style compounds. Examples: udgang (exit), udvikle (develop), udtale (pronounce).
ind- | in/into | The natural partner to ud-, meaning movement inward or inclusion, just like English "in-." It is often used where English would use "in-" or "into." Examples: indgang (entrance), indkøb (shopping), indflytning (move-in).
over- | over/too much | Works exactly like English "over-" to mean above, across, or excessive. Because the mapping is so clean, guessing the meaning of new over- words is usually reliable. Examples: oversætte (translate, lit. "over-set"), overraske (surprise), overholde (comply with).
under- | under/below | Parallel to English "under-" in almost every sense, including hierarchy and insufficiency. A great cheat code for learners because intuition from English transfers directly. Examples: undervise (teach), understrege (underline), undgå (avoid, though slightly irregular).
mis- | wrongly/badly | Identical in form and meaning to English "mis-," signalling that something is done incorrectly or badly. This is one of the easiest Danish prefixes for English speakers. Examples: misforstå (misunderstand), mislykkes (fail), mistro (distrust).
sam- | together/co- | Related to English "same" and German "zusammen," this prefix bundles things or people together. It is the Danish go-to equivalent of English "co-" or "together." Examples: samarbejde (cooperation), samtale (conversation), samle (collect/gather).
# Suffixes
-ing | action/process noun | Directly cognate with English "-ing," it forms nouns from verbs describing an action or its result. The connection makes these words very easy to absorb. Examples: læsning (reading), træning (training), udvikling (development).
-else | abstract noun | Turns verbs and adjectives into abstract nouns, roughly like English "-ness" or "-ance." It is one of the most productive suffixes in formal and everyday Danish alike. Examples: følelse (feeling), forståelse (understanding), oplevelse (experience).
-hed | state/quality noun | The direct cognate of English "-hood" and German "-heit," it creates abstract nouns from adjectives describing a state or quality. Examples: frihed (freedom), skønhed (beauty), sandhed (truth).
-ig | adjective from noun | Functions like English "-y" or "-ic," converting nouns into adjectives. It is extremely common and usually signals "having the quality of." Examples: venlig (friendly), rolig (calm), farlig (dangerous).
-lig | adjective/adverb | Related to English "-ly" and "-like," this suffix creates adjectives meaning "in the manner of" or "having the nature of." Many -lig adjectives also become adverbs unchanged. Examples: naturlig (natural), daglig (daily), mulig (possible).
-er | agent/person | Identical in function to English "-er," naming the person or thing that performs an action. You will recognize this pattern instantly. Examples: lærer (teacher), arbejder (worker), sanger (singer).
-tion/-sion | abstract process noun | Borrowed directly from Latin through French, just like in English, so thousands of these words are near-identical. The Danish pronunciation shifts the vowel but the spelling is a reliable bridge. Examples: nation (nation), situation (situation), funktion (function).
-løs | without/-less | The direct equivalent of English "-less," negating a noun to mean "lacking" something. A very clean one-to-one mapping that makes guessing new words easy. Examples: hjælpeløs (helpless), arbejdsløs (unemployed), tankeløs (thoughtless).
-isk | adjective of origin/type | Cognate with English "-ish" and "-ic," it forms adjectives relating to nationalities, fields, or character types. One of the most recognizable suffixes for English speakers. Examples: dansk (Danish), praktisk (practical), typisk (typical).
-skab | collective/abstract noun | Comparable to English "-ship" or "-hood," it forms nouns denoting a state, relationship, or collective. The family resemblance to "ship" (as in "friendship") is real and helpful. Examples: venskab (friendship), samfund (society, though compound), landskab (landscape).
# Roots and stems
-arbejd- | work | The core root for all things work-related, recognizable once you know it. It connects a whole cluster of everyday words and is worth memorising early. Examples: arbejde (work/job), arbejder (worker), samarbejde (cooperation).
-klar- | clear/ready | Carries two related meanings: clarity and readiness, similar to how English "clear" can mean both obvious and prepared. Examples: klar (clear/ready), forklare (explain), afklare (clarify).
-gå- | go/walk | This highly productive root appears in many compound and prefixed verbs, much like English "go" in phrasal verbs. Learning it unlocks a large family of words. Examples: gå (go/walk), indgå (enter into), foregå (take place).
-tid- | time | The central root for time-related vocabulary, cognate with English "tide" in its original sense of "time" (as in "yuletide"). Examples: tid (time), tidlig (early), nutid (present tense, lit. "now-time").
-sted- | place | Means "place" or "location" and shows up constantly in compound words, parallel to English "-stead" as in "homestead." Examples: sted (place), hjemsted (hometown), i stedet for (instead of).
# Linking elements
-s- | (linking genitive) | This little connector appears between parts of compound nouns, acting as a linking element and sometimes reflecting an old genitive. English has no direct equivalent but it is easy to spot once you know it. Examples: arbejdsdag (workday), landsby (village, lit. "land-s-by"), vindueskarm (windowsill).
-e- | (linking vowel) | A short connector vowel inserted between elements in compounds purely for pronunciation smoothness. It has no meaning of its own but its absence can make a compound feel awkward. Examples: grøntsager (vegetables, lit. "green-e-things"), dannebrog (the Danish flag), håndværker (craftsman).
